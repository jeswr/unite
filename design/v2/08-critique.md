# 08 — Adversarial self-critique (v2's own attacks, kept)

Same rule as design/06: attacks recorded permanently, with what the design
does about each and the honest residual. v1's C1–C10 all still apply to v2
(the engine and governance are unchanged); these are the *new* attacks the
v2 surface creates.

## C-v2-1 — Warmth is a persuasion technology

**Attack.** Everything the research synthesis recommends — mirroring,
relational register, felt micro-outcomes, invitation framing — is also the
toolkit of every manipulative growth product ever built. v2 uses measured
psychological levers to keep people talking; calling the levers "warmth"
doesn't change what they are. The Moon (2000) reciprocity result is cited
as an antipattern, but the mirror IS an elicitation device.

**Design response.** Three structural constraints separate support from
extraction: (a) the levers pull toward *no commercial objective* — there is
no engagement metric anywhere in the system to optimize, no ads, no
retention KPI, and the covenant bans the growth toolkit (P10) — warmth
with nothing to sell is hospitality; (b) every elicited datum lands in the
*person's own pod* under their ODRL policy, and the reveal test (01 §3) is
applied to each mechanism individually; (c) the notetaker's warmth budget
is capped by P9 — no intimacy simulation, no reciprocal self-disclosure,
warmth attaches to the humans and the shared picture.

**Residual.** Real. "We use the levers, but for you" is exactly what every
platform says. The only durable answer is the checkability (open engine,
pods, seams) plus exit rights — the same transparency-plus-exit answer as
v1's C8, and it is partial for the same reason: most people will never
check. Stated, not solved.

## C-v2-2 — The disclosure can be true and still unfelt

**Attack.** One warm handshake + seams satisfies the letter of every
citation, but attention is finite: most participants will never tap a seam,
never read How-unite-listens, and will experience v2 as "a nice chat" while
a clustering engine routes their social world. Consent-that-scrolls-past is
the cookie-banner failure with better prose.

**Design response.** The design deliberately spends *use-moments* on
re-disclosure: the mirror shows the inference itself (not a description of
it) every few turns; consent moments name the flow at the exact boundary
(02 §7); the notebook makes the model's whole content inspectable in the
person's own words. The system re-teaches what it is through its ordinary
operation — that is stronger than any document.

**Residual.** Between the mirror and the notebook there remains machinery
(composition, routing) a passive participant never consciously meets. The
"why this circle?" seam exists, but nobody is forced through it. Judged
acceptable because the un-met machinery decides *exposure order*, never
content or attribution — but "acceptable" is this design's judgment, and an
expert reviewer should re-judge it.

## C-v2-3 — Conversational elicitation may produce worse engine data

**Attack.** v1's deck yields dense, uniform tri-state votes — the matrix
the clustering/bridging math is fixture-proven on. v2 harvests resonance
from reaction gestures scattered through conversation: sparser, biased
toward what the router chose to surface, contaminated by social presence
(disagreeing with Rosa's face attached costs more than tapping a card).
The same warmth that raises participation may lower signal quality, and
the bridging math's guarantees are only as good as its matrix.

**Design response.** (a) The router *is* the v1 deck (03 §3) — coverage
logic (own-cluster-unseen-first, exposure spreading) is preserved, so
sparsity is bounded by the same active-routing that Pol.is relies on;
(b) the private "actually, I don't" tap (04 §4) and the post-reaction
distribution keep dissent cheap; (c) the letter's one-tap resonance adds a
low-social-pressure channel; (d) the demo's characterization fixtures run
the v2 elicitation path into the same expected outputs as v1's.

**Residual.** Untested empirically — this is the design's biggest open
empirical bet, and it is exactly the kind of question the recruited
experts (06 §6.3a) exist to design a measurement for. If conversational
elicitation degrades the matrix badly, v2's fallback is admitting more
deck-like moments into the flow (the costume gets thinner); that fallback
is acceptable and stated.

## C-v2-4 — Circle composition is invented mechanism wearing cited clothes

**Attack.** 04 §2 assembles sortition logic, contact theory, and the
existing clustering into a composition algorithm no study has validated.
Its failure modes are predictable: token-minority seating (one cluster-B
person alone among five cluster-A neighbours — Allport's equal-status
condition violated by composition itself), vacuous constraints at small n,
and the engine quietly deciding everyone's social graph.

**Design response.** Flagged as unvalidated in 04 §6 with the min-cluster-
floor guard; the token-minority case gets a hard rule at build time
(never exactly one member of a cluster in a circle — pairs minimum, which
the composition fixture asserts); composition is seam-carried and
documented (the Freeman rule).

**Residual.** A validated composition procedure does not exist in the
literature at this granularity; building one IS part of the research
agenda the pitch recruits for. Until then circle composition is the
design's most powerful un-reviewed lever. Named honestly.

## C-v2-5 — The demo sells an experience the deterministic scribe can't deliver

**Attack.** The five-minute arc depends on mirrors that feel accurately
heard; the shipped demo's scribe is a template engine plus canned persona
mirrors (06 §4). Either the demo quietly scripts the visitor into the
canned paths (and the "nothing is staged" line becomes false-adjacent), or
free-text visitors get clunky mirrors and the core feeling fails.

**Design response.** The honesty line is scoped precisely — *computation*
is real, *personas* are staged, and the scribe's nature is disclosed on its
own seam. The arc leans on the correction affordance ("close — let me fix
it") exactly because a mediocre draft plus a graceful fix still delivers
feeling-heard (the repair IS the responsiveness — Ashktorab). The pitch
names the live-LLM scribe as deferred work (07 §7).

**Residual.** A visitor who types something the template mangles may still
bounce with the wrong conclusion. Mitigation is copywriting and the
prominent fix-path, not a solution. Accepted for a prototype.

## C-v2-6 — Same-session summaries can lead the room

**Attack.** The living summary updates as people talk (the Remesh loop).
A summary that names a theme early *makes* that theme the room's agenda —
the notetaker's selection becomes soft agenda-setting power (v1's C1
mapper-power critique, relocated to the surface and made faster).

**Design response.** The summary only surfaces themes above the
k-threshold and only phrases them per `characterizeReception` verdicts
(computed, never editorial); "still forming" items are labeled and
unweighted; elicit-before-expose keeps a member's first take on any topic
uncontaminated; and the summary's seam exposes the derivation.

**Residual.** Ordering and phrasing within those constraints still carry
framing power — ranking IS power (v1 C8's words). unite's answer remains
transparency + determinism + exit, not neutrality. Unchanged, restated.

## C-v2-7 — Two surfaces, one truth: drift risk

**Attack.** v1's instrument views and v2's warm surfaces render the same
state through different idioms. Any divergence — a summary that says
"we're circling agreement" while the room view says `open` — is a
credibility wound discovered by exactly the behind-the-curtain visitors
the pitch invites to look.

**Design response.** Both surfaces read the same engine outputs (07 §1
forbids surface-local recomputation of verdicts); the verdict→phrasing map
is one module (`v2/seams.ts` + digest), fixture-pinned so every
`ReceptionVerdict` has exactly one v2 register; the behind-the-curtain
page renders both from one session state, making drift a test failure
rather than a discovery.

**Residual.** Copy edits are the drift vector fixtures can't fully pin
(a warmer adjective can overstate a verdict). The covenant review in the
demo walkthrough is the manual guard. Low, but standing.

## C-v2-8 — v2 defers the hard governance to vibes

**Attack.** v1's ceremony (quorum chips, steward signing, tier strips) was
ugly but *legible governance*. v2 hides tiers and signatures behind warm
introductions — which is how informal elites happen: the participant who
never sees the trust machinery cannot contest it.

**Design response.** Governance surfaces are hidden from *flow*, not from
*participants*: the notebook links every governance fact that touches you
(your tier, who stewards your community, what they may do); signing and
issuance keep their explicit v1 UIs (they are FOR governance actors);
published artifacts keep tier stratification and method-provenance labels
verbatim (those are output properties, P-covenant-exempt by design).

**Residual.** A warm surface still lowers the ambient visibility of who
holds power, full stop. The compensation is discoverability, which is
weaker than v1's in-your-face displays. This is a genuine trade the
maintainer should weigh explicitly — flagged as an open question.
